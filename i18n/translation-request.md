[explore.s3.eyebrow] Stage 3 of 10
[explore.s3.title] Inside the mouse brain
[explore.s3.body] This is the mesh of an actual mouse brain from the Allen Institute. The dots inside are placeholders for some of the ~70 million neurons that live in here.
[explore.s4.eyebrow] Stage 4 of 10
[explore.s4.title] Primary visual cortex
[explore.s4.body] Where the eye meets the brain. About a teaspoon of tissue at the back of the cortex, the first place signals from the retina turn into something the rest of the brain can use. Sight begins to become perception. The cells you'll meet next all came from this region.
[explore.s5.eyebrow] Stage 5 of 10
[explore.s5.title] A piece of cortex
[explore.s5.body] MICrONS reconstructed about a cubic millimeter of this region. Inside that cube: roughly 200,000 cells (neurons + glia), wired together by ~523 million synapses. Ten of those cells are shown here, drag to look around.
[explore.s6.eyebrow] Stage 6 of 10
[explore.s6.title] A neuron
[explore.s6.body] One cell, thousands of connections. Its branches, the dendrites, take signals in; its axon sends them out to other cells. Wire millions together and you get a network that holds thought and memory.
[explore.s7.eyebrow] Stage 7 of 10
[explore.s7.title] One synapse
[explore.s7.body] An axon reaches in from far away to touch the blue cell, a pyramidal neuron deep in the cortex. Where they meet is a synapse. The blue cell is excitatory: its signal urges the next cells to fire too.
[explore.s8.eyebrow] Stage 8 of 10
[explore.s8.title] Action potential
[explore.s8.body] A pulse races down the axon, reaches the blue cell, which fires its own. Here one synapse is shown; cells can have hundreds to thousands. Your brain sends tens of billions of these every second.
[explore.s9.eyebrow] Stage 9 of 10
[explore.s9.title] Activity
[explore.s9.body] Now multiply that one signal by hundreds of cells, all firing at once. These are real pyramidal neurons from the MICrONS cubic millimeter dataset, glowing in time with their measured calcium activity while a mouse watched a movie. Drag to rotate.

[teach.intro] This is a free, no-login tour of the real human and fly brain, built from actual scientific reconstructions, not illustrations. It works on any device, from a projector to a phone. Here are a few ways to use it with a class, plus questions to spark discussion.
[teach.print] Print this guide
[teach.ways.h2] Three ways to use it
[teach.way1.title] Project it
[teach.way1.body] Put the guided zoom on the big screen and travel from a whole brain down to a single synapse together. It auto-advances, so it runs itself while you narrate.
[teach.way1.cta] Open the wall view
[teach.way2.title] Explore the numbers
[teach.way2.body] Walk through how big a brain really is: 86 billion neurons, 2 million km of wiring, the folds, the fat, the speed. Every figure is sourced and turned into something you can picture.
[teach.way2.cta] Brains by the numbers
[teach.way3.title] Quiz them
[teach.way3.body] Five quick guess-the-number questions. Great as a warm-up or an exit ticket, and every answer reveals a real, surprising fact.
[teach.way3.cta] Try the quiz
[teach.discuss.h2] Discussion questions
[teach.discuss.intro] Open-ended, no single right answer. Good for a think-pair-share, a journal prompt, or a whole-class talk.
[teach.discuss.q1] Your brain is only 2% of your body weight but uses about 20% of your energy. Why might such a small organ be so expensive to run?
[teach.discuss.q2] Scientists have completely mapped a fly's brain (139,255 neurons) but not a mouse's or a human's. Why do you think a bigger brain is so much harder to map?
[teach.discuss.q3] The wiring in one human brain would stretch about 2 million km. What does a number that big actually mean to you? How could you picture it?
[teach.discuss.q4] What is one thing about the brain that surprises you? What question do you want to ask about the brain?
